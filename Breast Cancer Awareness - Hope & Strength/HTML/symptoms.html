Symptoms & Early Detection

Know the signs and take action early

Warning Signs to Watch For

New Lump

A new lump or mass in the breast or underarm area. Most lumps are not cancerous, but should be checked by a doctor.

üî¥

Breast Swelling

Swelling of all or part of the breast, even if no distinct lump is felt.

üî¥

Skin Changes

Dimpling, puckering, or redness of the breast skin. Sometimes described as an "orange peel" texture.

üî¥

Nipple Changes

Nipple turning inward, discharge (other than breast milk), or scaling/crusting of the nipple area.

üî¥

Breast Pain

Persistent pain in the breast or nipple area, though pain is not usually a sign of breast cancer.

üî¥

Size Changes

Any change in the size or shape of the breast, or noticeable asymmetry between breasts.

How to Perform a Self-Examination

1

Visual Inspection

Stand in front of a mirror with your arms at your sides. Look for any changes in size, shape, or appearance. Check for dimpling, puckering, or changes in skin texture.

2

Raise Your Arms

Raise your arms and look for the same changes. Check if both breasts move in the same way.

3

Check for Discharge

Gently squeeze each nipple and check for any discharge (clear, milky, or bloody fluid).

4

Feel While Lying Down

Lie down and use your right hand to feel your left breast, and vice versa. Use a firm, smooth touch with the pads of your fingers, keeping them flat and together.

5

Cover Entire Breast

Use a circular motion covering the entire breast from top to bottom, side to side. Use different pressure levels (light, medium, firm) to feel all breast tissue.

6

Feel While Standing

Finally, feel your breasts while standing or sitting. Many women find it easier to do this in the shower when the skin is wet and slippery.

When to See a Doctor

Important: If you notice any of these symptoms, don't panic. Most breast changes are not cancer. However, it's important to see a healthcare provider promptly for evaluation.

Early detection saves lives. Don't wait - schedule an appointment if you have concerns.

Diagnostic Tests

Mammogram

An X-ray of the breast used to detect tumors and abnormalities. Recommended annually for women 40+.

Ultrasound

Uses sound waves to create images of breast tissue. Often used to further evaluate abnormalities found on mammograms.

MRI

Magnetic resonance imaging provides detailed images. Used for high-risk patients or to evaluate suspicious findings.

Biopsy

A procedure to remove a sample of tissue for examination under a microscope to determine if cancer cells are present.
