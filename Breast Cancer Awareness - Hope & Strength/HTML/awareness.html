Breast Cancer Awareness

Empowering knowledge for prevention and early detection

Prevention Strategies

01

Maintain a Healthy Weight

Being overweight or obese increases the risk of breast cancer, especially after menopause. Regular exercise and a balanced diet can help maintain a healthy weight.

02

Stay Physically Active

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity per week. Regular exercise can reduce breast cancer risk by 10-20%.

03

Limit Alcohol Intake

Even small amounts of alcohol can increase risk. If you choose to drink, limit to no more than one drink per day.

04

Healthy Diet

Eat plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins. Limit processed foods and red meat consumption.

05

Breastfeed if Possible

Breastfeeding can lower breast cancer risk, especially when continued for a year or more.

06

Know Your Family History

Talk to your family about cancer history and discuss genetic testing with your doctor if you have a strong family history.

What is breast cancer?

+

Breast cancer is a disease where cells in the breast grow uncontrollably. It usually starts in the ducts (that carry milk) or lobules (that produce milk) and can spread to nearby tissues or other parts of the body if not treated early.

What causes breast cancer?

+

The exact cause isn't known, but it develops when genetic mutations cause breast cells to grow abnormally. Risk factors such as age, family history, hormone exposure, obesity, alcohol use, and radiation exposure can increase the likelihood.

Who is at risk of getting breast cancer?

+

Anyone can get breast cancer, but the risk is higher for:

  • Women over 40
  • Those with a family history of breast or ovarian cancer
  • People with BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutations
  • Those with early menstruation or late menopause
  • People who use hormone replacement therapy

What are the early signs and symptoms of breast cancer?

+

Common symptoms include:

  • A lump or thickening in the breast or underarm
  • Changes in breast size, shape, or appearance
  • Dimpling or puckering of the skin
  • Nipple discharge (especially if bloody)
  • Pain or tenderness in the breast
  • Nipple turning inward (inversion)

How can breast cancer be detected early?

+

Early detection methods include:

  • Self-breast examination: Checking your breasts regularly for any changes.
  • Clinical breast exam: Performed by a healthcare professional.
  • Mammogram: An X-ray of the breast that detects lumps before they are felt.

Can men get breast cancer?

+

Yes. Although rare, men can develop breast cancer since they also have breast tissue. Symptoms and treatment are similar to those in women.

How is breast cancer treated?

+

Treatment depends on the stage and type of cancer and may include:

  • Surgery: To remove the tumor or entire breast (mastectomy).
  • Radiation therapy: Uses high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells.
  • Chemotherapy: Uses drugs to kill or stop the growth of cancer cells.
  • Hormone therapy: Blocks hormones that fuel certain cancers.
  • Targeted therapy: Focuses on specific molecules involved in cancer growth.

Can breast cancer be prevented?

+

It cannot always be prevented, but risk can be reduced by:

  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Exercising regularly
  • Limiting alcohol
  • Avoiding smoking
  • Eating a balanced diet
  • Regular screening and self-checks

What are the stages of breast cancer?

+

Breast cancer is staged from 0 to IV:

  • Stage 0: Non-invasive (cancer cells remain in the ducts).
  • Stages I‚ÄìIII: Cancer is invasive but limited to the breast or nearby lymph nodes.
  • Stage IV: Cancer has spread (metastasized) to other organs.

Does having a lump always mean breast cancer?

+

No. Many breast lumps are benign (non-cancerous), such as cysts or fibroadenomas. However, any new lump should be checked by a healthcare provider to rule out cancer.

What is the survival rate for breast cancer?

+

When detected early (localized), the 5-year survival rate is over 90%. Early diagnosis and improved treatments have greatly increased survival chances.

Can I live a normal life after breast cancer treatment?

+

Yes. Many people go on to live long, healthy lives after treatment. Regular follow-ups, a balanced diet, exercise, and emotional support are key to recovery.

Screening Guidelines

20-39 Years

Perform monthly breast self-exams and have a clinical breast exam every 1-3 years.

40-49 Years

Annual clinical breast exams and discuss mammography with your doctor. Consider annual mammograms.

50+ Years

Annual mammograms and clinical breast exams. Continue monthly self-examinations.
